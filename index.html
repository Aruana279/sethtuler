Our regional focus:

The landscape of Massachusetts ref lects complex dynamics within and among urban and rural communities. Choices made by individuals, groups, and government actors at local, state, regional, and national scales to provide food and drinking water, energy, economic development, housing, transportation, conservation and protection of ecological services, recreation, and historical preservation create mosaics of land uses that change over time. Transitions can be dramatic: New England was mostly forested before colonization by Europeans, who transformed it into a landscape dominated by fields, pastures, and woodlots. Now, it is both the most heavily forested region of the US and, in its south, among the most densely populated regions. There is also increasing loss of undeveloped lands.

Now, Massachusetts has a diversity of land covers under significant pressure from development in urban, peri-urban, and rural communities.

It is the 3rd most densely populated and 8th most densely forested state.

About 21% of the stateâ€™s land area is developed, while approximately 27% is permanently protected, leaving 52% of the land in the state neither developed nor protected.

Within its borders are three of the four largest cities in New England (Boston, Worcester, and Springfield), along with 26 mid-sized Gateway Cities. Connections among the urban cities and rural communities are varied and deep.

Characterize groups and networks addressing land use choices in MA

We are distributing a survey to organizations in Massachusetts engaging with land-use management, planning, or advocacy in pursuit of important sustainability, climate mitigation, and climate adaptation goals. We have sent it out to over 1200 groups.

The purpose of the survey is to document information about civil society groups working in at local, regional, and state levels in Massachusetts on issues that impact land use decisions and management:
Where they are located
What kinds of issues they work on
How they work on the issues (e.g., advocacy, planning, coordinating partnerships, managing land, provide funding, research)
Who the group collaborates with to achieve its goals that can affect land use or landscapes in Massachusetts and the nature of the collaborative relationships

Identify and explore potential trade-offs and synergisms among the goals of actors and networks

A series of facilitated workshops will be held, 2-3 hours each to:
Present and discuss results from the survey and interviews. Explore how specific land-use related policies and programs lead to trade-offs, co-benefits, and risks associated with, for example, health, equity and racial justice, climate resilience, functional and resilient ecosystems, food security, and economic security and vitality.

The workshops will be in different regions of Massachusetts and focus on different contexts (i.e., primarily rural, urban center with rural surrounding communities, primarily urban, coastal with nitrogen loading concerns). We will structure the workshops using the Vulnerability, Consequences, and Adaptation Planning Scenarios (VCAPS) process.

Co-develop models and identify measures for integrated land use governance goals

Participatory modeling will be used to explore with stakeholders scenarios related to land use policies and programs. A scenario might address, for example, competing visions for forest management in light of trade-offs associated with increased demand for local timber for regional home construction and carbon sequestration and habitat preservation goals. Participatory modeling can promote social learning and capacity building by helping to develop shared understandings, a common agenda, framing effects, and a set of measures for assessing sustainability, well-being, and resilience in the connected urban-rural region.

Participatory modeling workshops are planned for the summer of 2022. If you are interested in learning more or possibly participating, please contact us. Funding will be provided to support participation.
